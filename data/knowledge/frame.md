The structure that carries the loads and the surfaces the pilot moves to steer it.

- Structure & Loads :: How the airframe is built and how flight loads travel through it to the ground and back.
  - Fuselage Construction :: Truss (welded tube), monocoque (skin carries all load) and semi-monocoque (skin + stringers + bulkheads) — the near-universal metal airplane.
  - Wing Structure :: Spars carry bending, ribs give the airfoil shape, stringers and skin carry the rest. Cantilever (internal spar) vs strut-braced; a "wet wing" is a sealed integral tank.
  - Empennage :: The tail: vertical stabilizer + rudder for yaw, horizontal stabilizer + elevator (or a one-piece stabilator) for pitch. It provides the stabilizing tail-down force. -> longitudinal-stability "provides pitch stability"
  - Load Paths & Fatigue :: Limit load = the most you should ever pull; ultimate = 1.5× that before failure. Repeated cycles fatigue metal — hence life limits and inspections. -> v-speeds-and-the-envelope "limited by the V-n diagram" -> airworthiness "inspection intervals"
- Primary Flight Controls :: The three that move the airplane about its three axes — roll, pitch and yaw.
  - Ailerons — Roll :: Move opposite each other to bank about the longitudinal axis. The down-going aileron adds lift and drag, so they generate adverse yaw. -> adverse-yaw "cause adverse yaw"
  - Elevator / Stabilator — Pitch :: Change the tail's angle of attack to pitch about the lateral axis. A stabilator is an all-moving surface with an anti-servo tab to add feel and prevent over-control.
  - Rudder — Yaw :: Controls yaw about the vertical axis — it coordinates turns and counters adverse yaw and P-factor. It is not what turns the airplane. -> p-factor "counters P-factor"
- Secondary Controls & High Lift :: Trim to relieve pressure, and the devices that reshape the wing for slow flight.
  - Trim Systems :: Trim tabs, anti-servo tabs, servo tabs or a movable stabilizer relieve the control-pressure so the pilot doesn't hold force. Set for hands-off flight at a target speed.
  - Flaps :: Plain, split, slotted and Fowler flaps raise the coefficient of lift (and drag), lowering stall speed so you can approach slower and steeper. -> stall "lower stall speed" -> induced-drag "add drag"
  - Leading-Edge Devices :: Slots and slats delay separation to a higher AoA for more usable lift; on jets they extend the low-speed envelope. Spoilers/speedbrakes do the opposite — kill lift and add drag.
- Control Mechanics :: How yoke and pedals actually move the surfaces, and what limits how fast you may fly.
  - Cables, Pushrods & Bellcranks :: Yoke and pedals drive the surfaces through cables, pushrods, bellcranks and stops. Control checks confirm "free and correct" before every flight.
  - Flutter & Balance :: Surfaces are mass- and aerodynamically balanced so they don't flutter — a destructive resonance that Vne is set below. Never exceed the red line, especially in rough air. -> v-speeds-and-the-envelope "why Vne exists"
- Landing Gear & Brakes :: What holds the airplane up on the ground and stops it.
  - Fixed vs Retractable :: Tricycle gear is stable and easy on the ground; tailwheel demands active directional control. Retractable gear cuts drag but adds a "gear-down" item that must never be skipped.
  - Brakes & Tires :: Independent hydraulic disc brakes give differential steering; hydroplaning on a wet runway and overheating on hard braking are the traps.
  - Shock Absorption :: Oleo (air/oil) struts, spring-steel legs or bungees absorb the touchdown and taxi loads — the reason a firm arrival isn't a structural one.
